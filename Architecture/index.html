Gothic

Gothic architecture developed in 12th-century France and is best known for its soaring verticality and dramatic visual impact. Key features include pointed arches, ribbed vaults, flying buttresses, and expansive stained-glass windows. These innovations allowed cathedrals to rise to unprecedented heights and become filled with light, symbolizing divine presence. In Paris, Gothic architecture is embodied in monumental works such as Notre-Dame Cathedral. Renaissance

Baroque

Baroque architecture emerged in late 16th-century Italy and spread across Europe as a response to the Renaissance. It is characterized by grandeur, theatricality, and dynamic ornamentation. Curved forms, elaborate domes, dramatic use of light and shadow, and intricate details define the style. In Paris, Baroque buildings often signified royal power and Catholic strength, with examples such as Les Invalides. The style is associated with the absolutist state and the Counter-Reformation, using architecture to impress, overwhelm, and emotionally engage the viewer. In urban settings, Baroque principles also shaped symmetrical avenues and dramatic public spaces that guided the viewer's eye and movement.
